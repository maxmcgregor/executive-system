# /how-to-use -- System Guide

This skill explains how the executive system works and why each piece matters. Use it when the user asks "how does this work?" or "what should I be doing?"

## The Philosophy

This system runs three feedback loops at three speeds:

- **Daily** -- Are you working on the right thing today?
- **Weekly** -- Are you making progress this week?
- **Monthly** -- Are you heading in the right direction this quarter?

Each loop catches different kinds of drift. Daily catches distraction. Weekly catches lost momentum. Monthly catches strategic misalignment. Together, they keep you honest without requiring constant vigilance.

## The Minimum Viable Habit Loop

Four touchpoints. Everything else is optional.

1. **`/start`** -- Begin each work session here. Takes 2-3 minutes. Reads your current goals, recent work, and active experiments, then helps you decide what to focus on. This is the habit that makes everything else work.

2. **`/log`** -- End each work session here. Takes 1-2 minutes. Captures what you did in a build log. These logs are the raw data that powers weekly reviews and board meetings. Short logs beat no logs. A single bullet point is enough.

3. **`/weekly-review`** -- Once a week, ideally Fridays. Takes 5-10 minutes. Compares your build logs against your goals and surfaces drift, stalled work, or positive momentum. Claude drafts the review -- you just add context.

4. **`/board-meeting`** -- Once a month, first Friday. Takes 30-60 minutes. Full strategic review with your board of advisors. Evaluates experiments, reviews patterns, triages ideas, and sets direction for the next month. This is the most important ritual.

## Why Each Cadence Matters

### /start
Two minutes that determine whether you spend the next few hours on what matters or what's loudest. Without it, the default is to open whatever feels urgent or interesting. With it, you start from your goals and choose deliberately.

### /log
Creates the data everything else runs on. Weekly reviews and board meetings are only as good as the build logs they read. The act of logging also forces a brief moment of honesty: did I do real work today?

### /journal
Optional, but powerful. Surfaces what's underneath the tasks -- frustrations, tensions, ideas that need space to develop. The system reads your journal entries during board meetings to detect patterns you might not notice in the day-to-day. Use it when something's brewing.

### /weekly-review
The shortest accountability loop. Catches drift within days instead of months. If you're working hard but not making progress on your goals, this is where that shows up. If you're on track, the data confirms it -- no guessing.

### /board-meeting
The strategic zoom-out. Prevents spending months heading in the wrong direction. Your board challenges your thinking from multiple angles. Experiments get evaluated honestly. Ideas get triaged instead of impulsively pursued. Direction gets set deliberately.

### /capture
The guard rail against shiny object syndrome. When a new idea strikes, capture it and move on. The board evaluates it monthly. This separates the thrill of a new idea from the decision to pursue it.

## Tips

- **Be honest.** The system works by comparing what you said you'd do with what you actually did. If you're not honest in your logs and reviews, the system can't help you.
- **Short logs beat no logs.** A single bullet point is infinitely more useful than a skipped day. Don't let perfectionism kill the habit.
- **Depth scales with you.** Every interaction has a low floor (a sentence or two) and a high ceiling (go as deep as you want). Start minimal, add depth as the habit sticks.
- **The system adapts.** Your profile, goals, and board members can change anytime. The system is a living tool, not a rigid framework.
- **You're the decision maker.** The system presents information and options. You choose. It's a peer, not a manager.

## When You Present This

Present the information conversationally, not as a wall of text. Lead with the minimum viable habit loop, then explain why each piece matters if the user wants to go deeper. Keep it practical.
